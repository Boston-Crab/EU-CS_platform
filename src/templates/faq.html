Frequently Asked Questions


Who is the EU-Citizen.Science platform for?

Our community is open to anyone interested in citizen science - our vision for the platform is to encourage the democratization of science via the wide establishment and appreciation of citizen science approaches across Europe.

We hope to become the central reference point for anyone setting up or running a citizen science project - such as practitioners, researchers, educators, communities and citizens, anyone supporting the ecosystem of citizen science projects - such as policy makers and funding bodies, and anyone interested in the outcomes and impact of citizen science - such as decision makers, the press, and society as a whole. It doesn’t matter if you have years of experience or are completely new to citizen science.

You can read more about our assessment of our stakeholders, community members, and target audience in the project Stakeholders,Network & Community Mapping Report and in the project Engagement and Community- building Plan.

What are the features of the EU-Citizen.Science platform?

This alpha release of the platform (launched in April 2020) has a Resources section where you can browse and search for resources that are useful for planning and running citizen science projects, and a projects section where you can browse and search for citizen science projects to learn from other’s experience and collaborate. If you sign up as a member of the community, you will be able to follow projects, add resources to your library, and add your own projects and resources to those sections.

You can read about our platform development and release planning in the project Platform Functionality Requirements & Specification Report

Are the resources hosted on the EU-Citizen.Science platform?

No, we don’t host any documents, files, or software on our own platform. When a profile is created for a resource, it points to the location where that resource is stored or published, and project profiles point to the external website of that project. We strongly recommend the use of permanent repositories and permalinks, so that links do not become broken over time.

I can’t find the type of resource that I’m looking for, can you help?

While the alpha release of the Platform is in the content-gathering phase, we will still be missing many of the rich citizen science resources that exist already. But it may be that what you are looking for does not exist yet. Please send us a message at eucitsci@mfn.berlin and we will do our best to help you find it, or initiate the creation of it!

Can I add a project or resource of my own?

Yes, we are currently in the content-gathering phase with this alpha release of the platform, and therefore actively invite all members of the community to share their citizen science projects and resources. To add your project to the Project section, or your resource to the Resource section, you will first need to Sign Up and Log In to the platform.

What types of resources can I submit?

Resources can include documents such as guidelines, reports, policy briefs, and protocols; technical tools such as software or hardware; and other file formats such as videos or podcasts.

The resource should be relevant to the initiation, planning, execution, or closure of citizen science projects, either for experienced or new practitioners.

The resource should also meet a range of quality factors such as whether it is easy to access, is structured clearly, is easy to read (or hear or view), and easy to understand. Read our Quality Guidelines here.

What types of projects can I submit?

Projects can be short-term or long-term citizen science initiatives, projects with an end date, or even one-day ‘Blitz’ events.

Citizen science is a flexible concept which can be adapted and applied within diverse situations and disciplines, and represents a range of approaches and historical practices for public participation in scientific research. Citizen science projects can involve voluntary participants (citizen scientists) in many stages of the scientific process, from data collection and volunteer mapping, through data interpretation and analysis, to publication and dissemination of results.

For guidance on whether your project can be considered to be a citizen science project, please refer to the ECSA 10 Principles of Citizen Science.

How do I add a project or resource?

Once you have signed up and are logged in , go to the Resources page or the Project page, and look for the Add+ button under the search bar at the top of that page. You will be taken to a form that will ask you for a range of mandatory and option information.

This information will be used for creating the public profile of the project or resource on our platform. Please note that at first this profile will be marked as ‘Unmoderated’. Once a member of the EU-Citizen.Science project has reviewed your submission, it will be marked as ‘Moderated’, or we will contact you with questions about the resource or project.

How do you prevent dead links?

Encouraging Permalinks: In the form for submitting resources, alongside the metadata field for the link to the resource, we will strongly encourage the use of permalinks from hosting repositories such as Zenodo, OSF or the RIO Journal that create a permanent DOI for the resource, and support version management.
  • Users will be warned that resources with broken links will be delisted, but they can update the links from their resource dashboard at any time.
  • When a resource is delisted, the person who submitted it will receive an email informing them of this, the reason, and how they can address the issue and resubmit the resource.
Flagging Resources: All resources can be flagged by the community as having an issue that needs addressing, which includes broken links. These will then be delisted and the submitter of the resource will be informed about how to address the issue and encouraged to resubmit it.

Deadlink Checker: we will integrate a script to run software that checks for broken links such as Broken Link Checker by W-Shadow on a regular basis - likely once a month for the first year, and once a quarter thereafter. (Links go dead over a matter of years, not months, as server-support often gets pulled approximately 2-3 years after a project ends)

How do we deal with outdated resources & content changes?

Resource Dashboard: The registered user who has submitted a Resource has access to the Resource profiles that they have created via a dashboard. Resources can be updated at any time by providing a link to a new document. If the Resource is hosted on a platform such as Zenodo or OSF, then the version management can happen at that location and the link and DOI will remain constant. It is up to the community to keep their resources up to date.

Flagging Resources: All resources can be flagged by the community as having an issue that needs addressing, which includes resources that appear to be outdated. These will then be delisted and the submitter of the resource will be informed about how to address the issue and resubmit.

What is Citizen Science?

Citizen science actively involves the public in scientific research that generates new knowledge or understanding, and thus has the potential to bring together science, policy makers, and society as a whole in an impactful way.

As a core dimension of Open Science, it opens up the opportunity for all members of society to take an active role in research, innovation and the development of evidence-based policy, at local, national and EU levels. Citizen scientists can participate in many stages of the scientific process, from data collection and volunteer mapping, through data interpretation and analysis, to publication and dissemination of results.

Citizen science is a flexible concept which can be adapted and applied within diverse situations and disciplines, and represents a range of approaches and historical practices for public participation in scientific research. As a result, there are a range of definitions of citizen science, as can be seen in the Wikipedia entry for Citizen Science.

A set of key principles that underlie good practice in citizen science - the 10 Principles of Citizen Science - has been developed by the ‘Sharing best practice and building capacity’ working group of the European Citizen Science Association, led by the Natural History Museum London with input from many members of the Association.
